# What DHH taught me about staying unconventional

*Dev Propulsion Labs · December 2023*

David Heinemeier Hansson -- DHH -- is the creator of Ruby on Rails, co-founder of Basecamp and HEY, best-selling author, Le Mans class-winning racing driver, and one of the most opinionated people in tech. Victoria Melnikova sat down with him for Dev Propulsion Labs to talk about two decades of Rails, why he left the cloud, and what it means to build a company that refuses to follow the script.

## Twenty years of Rails and still shipping

Ruby on Rails turned the web development world upside down. When DHH first extracted it from Basecamp back in 2004, the idea that a single developer could build a full web application rapidly was borderline radical. Convention over configuration. Don't repeat yourself. These weren't just technical principles. They were a philosophy about how software should be built.

What strikes me about talking to DHH is that he's still as passionate about Rails now as he was two decades ago. This isn't someone resting on legacy. Rails 7 brought Hotwire, Turbo, and a whole new way of thinking about front-end development without drowning in JavaScript frameworks. At Evil Martians, we've been deep in the Rails ecosystem for years, and watching it continue to evolve is genuinely exciting. The framework keeps finding new ways to make developers productive without adding complexity.

## The cloud exodus that nobody expected

DHH made headlines when Basecamp left the cloud. The numbers were staggering: they were spending millions on AWS and realized they could own their own hardware for a fraction of the long-term cost. He didn't just quietly move infrastructure. He wrote about it publicly, in detail, with the receipts.

> "We saved over $1 million a year by leaving the cloud. And the performance is better."

Here's the thing. When DHH talks about leaving the cloud, he's not saying it's right for everyone. He's saying the default assumption -- that every company should be on AWS or Google Cloud -- deserves to be questioned. For companies with predictable workloads that have outgrown the elastic scaling phase, owning your hardware can make enormous financial sense. Victoria Melnikova has seen this across the startups she works with. The cloud is fantastic for getting started. But at scale, the economics can flip dramatically.

## Building a company that doesn't need venture capital

Basecamp has been profitable since almost the beginning. No venture capital. No board of directors pushing for hyper-growth. No pressure to exit. DHH and Jason Fried built the kind of company most people in Silicon Valley would say is impossible: calm, profitable, and sustainable for over two decades.

Victoria Melnikova asked about this because she talks to devtools founders constantly who feel trapped by the VC playbook. Raise money, grow at all costs, find an exit. DHH represents the counter-narrative. You can build a meaningful technology company, pay people well, ship great products, and do it without giving up control. The catch? You have to be comfortable being smaller. You have to resist the growth-at-all-costs pressure that defines so much of the tech industry.

Honestly, after talking to hundreds of startup founders, I find DHH's model more compelling than ever. Not because it's easy, but because the companies built this way tend to still be around a decade later. The ones that chased growth at all costs? Many of them aren't.

## Opinionated software for opinionated people

DHH doesn't build neutral tools. Rails is famously opinionated. It tells you where your files go, how your database maps to your objects, and what your URL structure should look like. Some developers hate this. DHH doesn't care.

> "If you try to please everyone, you end up pleasing no one. Make strong choices and let people opt out if they disagree."

This philosophy extends beyond code. HEY, the email service Basecamp launched, reimagined how email works from scratch. It doesn't have labels. It doesn't auto-sort. It forces you to screen new senders before they can reach your inbox. Love it or hate it -- and people feel strongly both ways -- it's a product with a point of view.

I think this is one of the most underrated lessons for devtools founders. The temptation is always to be flexible, to support every use case, to never alienate anyone. But the products that create real loyalty are the ones that make strong choices. Rails did that. HEY did that. And the developers who love those products really love them.

## The race car driver who writes code

DHH is also a Le Mans class-winning racing driver. I bring this up not as a fun fact but because it tells you something about how he approaches everything. Racing at Le Mans requires years of preparation, physical conditioning, and the willingness to push yourself to the absolute limit while managing enormous risk.

The same intensity shows up in his approach to technology. He doesn't half-commit to anything. When he decided to leave the cloud, he didn't just migrate -- he documented the entire journey and made it into a movement. When he disagreed with Apple's App Store policies, he testified before Congress. When he thought remote work was the future, he wrote multiple books about it years before COVID proved him right.

There's a pattern here that I find really valuable for founders: conviction creates momentum. You don't have to be right about everything. But you do have to believe in something strongly enough to actually do it, publicly, and deal with the consequences. DHH has been doing that for twenty years, and it has made both Rails and Basecamp what they are today.

## Why this conversation matters for devtools builders

This was one of Victoria Melnikova's earliest episodes of Dev Propulsion Labs, and it set a tone for everything that came after. DHH represents something rare in tech: a founder who has been building for over twenty years, who has stayed independent, who has maintained strong opinions in an industry that loves consensus, and who keeps shipping.

For the devtools founders I talk to every week, there's a crucial lesson here. You don't have to play the game everyone else is playing. You can be profitable without VC money. You can own your infrastructure. You can build opinionated software that not everyone likes. You can take strong public stances and survive the backlash. The key is consistency over time.

Rails is still here. Basecamp is still here. HEY is still here. In an industry where most products don't survive five years, twenty years of relevance is the strongest argument you can make. And DHH isn't slowing down.
