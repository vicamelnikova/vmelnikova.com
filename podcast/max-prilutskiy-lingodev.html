What Max Prilutskiy taught me about going from a hackathon to YC on cold calls and word of mouth

Max Prilutskiy is the CEO and co-founder of Lingo.dev, an AI-powered localization platform. He's been coding since he was nine years old in Belarus, sold a bootstrapped startup before this one, and started Lingo.dev at a hackathon with zero ideas. What I loved about our conversation is how much of his go-to-market came from simply getting on calls with people and listening.

Coding since nine, and a first exit before Lingo.dev

I'm Victoria Melnikova, and I have a soft spot for founders who started young. Max began coding at nine years old in Belarus, and by the time he got to Lingo.dev he had already built and sold a bootstrapped startup. That background shows up in how he operates: pragmatic, unattached to any single idea, and comfortable shipping before the plan is fully formed. Founders with an exit behind them tend to trust the process of discovery more than the brilliance of a first idea, and Max is a clean example of that.

A hackathon with zero ideas

Here's the detail I keep repeating to founders I work with: Lingo.dev started at a hackathon where Max had no idea what to build. Not a stealth vision, not a manifesto, just showing up and making something. The company that became an AI-powered localization platform emerged from that low-stakes, high-iteration setting. It's a useful counter to the myth that great companies begin with a singular insight. Sometimes they begin with a weekend and a willingness to try.

Cold exploration calls as a whole go-to-market

The part most relevant to my work was Max's go-to-market. He built it out of cold exploration calls, conversations where the goal is to understand the person on the other end rather than to close them. Those calls shaped what Lingo.dev became and how he talks about it. I spend a lot of my time pushing early founders to do exactly this, and it's still rare to meet someone who made customer discovery the actual engine of their strategy rather than a box to check.

Why word of mouth is still the only channel that works

Max's take on growth is refreshingly unglamorous: word of mouth remains the only reliable channel. For a developer-facing product, that tracks with everything I see. Developers trust other developers. No amount of paid acquisition substitutes for a peer saying "use this." The implication for founders is that the product and the early relationships are the marketing, and everything else is amplification at best.

What agent experience means for devtools in 2026

We closed on where developer tools are heading, and what agent experience really means in 2026. Localization is an interesting vantage point for that question, because it sits between code, content, and the humans who consume it in different languages, exactly the kind of workflow agents are starting to touch. Going from a hackathon to YC on the strength of cold calls and word of mouth, Max is a reminder that the fundamentals of building for developers haven't changed as much as the tooling around them has.
