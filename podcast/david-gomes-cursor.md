*Dev Propulsion Labs · February 2026*

I'm Victoria Melnikova, and this is a conversation with a Cursor product engineer who's lived inside SingleStore, Neon, and now Cursor. We talked about the asymmetric world of AI adoption, building engineering teams across continents, and why your next career move should be an IC role.

## The future is here. It's just wildly uneven

I moved to SF in March 2025 and immediately felt like everything was changing every single day. New terms, new models, new paradigms. But when Victoria Melnikova talks to developers outside this bubble (and I talk to a lot of them through Dev Propulsion Labs), many haven't even tried agentic coding. David confirmed what I've been sensing: around 50% of the world's software engineers still aren't using agentic coding tools in any meaningful way.

Meanwhile, the best engineers in the world, people like Jose Valim and Jared Sumner, are producing most of their code through agents. It's an asymmetric world. The tools aren't magic yet. They require practice, prompting skill, and understanding of rules, skills, and MCPs. David sees this as a massive market still to capture, which is exactly how someone at Cursor should be thinking about it.

> "It's basically like a new thing that you have to learn as a software engineer. It takes practice. It takes some amount of skill."

Honestly, I've seen this firsthand with the engineers I work with at Evil Martians. The senior developers who initially pushed back the hardest are now the ones getting the best results, once they took the leap and put in the reps.

## Even inside Cursor, nobody codes the same way

This surprised me. You'd think the people building the leading AI code editor would converge on one workflow. Nope. Some engineers at Cursor live in the CLI. Some run cloud agents from the web UI or their phone. David personally uses Cursor's Best-of-N feature, launching three Codex and three Opus models on the same task and picking the best output.

He doesn't see a path toward uniformity anytime soon, and I think he's right. Software engineers have always customized their environments obsessively. AI coding is no different. The implication for devtools companies is clear: you can't force a single workflow. You have to build for the diversity of how people actually work.

## The competitive programming kid who built a 50-person team back home

David grew up doing competitive programming in Portugal, dreamed of moving to SF since high school, and got there right after university. At SingleStore, he did something I find genuinely admirable. He convinced the company to open an engineering office in Portugal. He grew it from zero to 50 people.

His argument for Portugal as a first European office is practical and compelling: the right time zone (eight hours from SF), exceptional English skills (because the Portuguese don't dub TV, seriously, that's the reason), and a lower cost of living than Ireland or the UK. He believes his team actually shifted the salary market in Lisbon for top-tier engineers. Before SingleStore, there weren't many US companies hiring locally. Now CloudFlare, Datadog, PagerDuty, and Docker all have offices there.

I lived in Lisbon for a few years, so this resonated with me. Portugal has incredible talent. It just needed someone to open the door.

## Inside Neon's billion-dollar exit to Databricks

David worked at Neon under Nikita Shamgunov. It was his second time working for Nikita, after SingleStore. He describes Neon's product simply: "Postgres, but easy and cheap." The revenue was growing like a rocket. The $1B acquisition by Databricks was, in his words, "one for the books."

> "The value that Neon was acquired for is just insane. $1 billion for a company that had been created just a few years before the exit."

He credits Nikita for negotiating to keep Neon as its own brand and product post-acquisition. That's not common. Having talked to Stas Kelvich from Neon on a later episode of Dev Propulsion Labs, Victoria Melnikova can confirm this was a genuinely unusual deal structure. It says a lot about how strong Neon's product-led growth was that Databricks wanted to preserve it intact.

## Not everyone needs to care about the business, and that's fine

I asked David whether engineers should understand business goals. His answer was more nuanced than I expected. He used to get frustrated when engineers didn't care about competitors or revenue. He's since learned that teams work best when they're diverse in orientation.

Some people are obsessed with the technology. Others focus on the product and market. The key is directing each person's energy toward work that matches their strengths, not forcing everyone into the same mold. Let me tell you, this is advice that more engineering leaders need to hear. I've seen too many startups try to turn every engineer into a mini product manager. It doesn't work.

## The engineer-manager pendulum, and why his next company will be his own

David references Charity Majors' blog post about alternating between IC and management roles throughout a career. He's done exactly that. IC to manager at SingleStore, IC to manager at Neon, now IC at Cursor. He chose to be an IC right now because he wants to put in reps on AI coding before managing again.

When I asked about starting his own company, he said something that made me laugh: "My next company is gonna be my own, but I just keep getting offers I can't refuse." Cursor was one of those offers. He was already using the product all day long and had always wanted to work on IDEs.

Here's what Victoria Melnikova took away from this conversation: we're in a wildly asymmetric moment. Half the world's engineers haven't adopted AI coding. The ones who have are pulling further and further ahead. The gap is real, and it's growing. If you're still on the fence, put in the reps. It's a learnable skill, not magic.
