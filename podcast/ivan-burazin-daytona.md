# What Ivan Burazin taught me about walking away from revenue to bet on agents

*Dev Propulsion Labs · November 2025*

I'm Victoria Melnikova, and on this episode of Dev Propulsion Labs I sat down with a founder who has been building cloud development environments since 2009, a full decade before the market was ready. When the AI agent wave hit, he didn't dip a toe in. He walked away from $300K ARR on New Year's Eve and rebuilt everything from scratch.

- YouTube: https://youtu.be/6WCwO_oGsBY
- Show notes: https://evilmartians.com/events/ivan-burazin-ceo-of-daytona-walking-from-300k-arr-to-build-for-agents

Victoria Melnikova first connected with Ivan back in 2023, when Daytona was just an idea. He was still figuring things out. And now here he is, doubling revenue every month, buying servers at a pace that would make your head spin, and drowning in master service agreements. The speed of this transformation is honestly wild.

## Composable computers for agents (not sandboxes)

Ivan is clear about something: the word "sandbox" undersells what Daytona actually builds. What they create are composable computers for AI agents. At creation time, an agent can specify CPUs, RAM, disk, GPU, even the operating system. Linux, Windows, Mac. It's not just a place to execute code safely. It's a full computer that the agent can configure on demand.

Here's the scale that blew my mind. Think about ChatGPT. Millions of users. Each interaction is a new agent. Each agent might spin up a computer. Now multiply that across every company building agents. Ivan puts it simply: the number of agents will be the number of humans to the power of ten.

> "The number of agents will essentially be the number of humans to the power of ten. They will need this new primitive to be able to get their job done."

I've seen a lot of founders pitch big numbers. Ivan is living them. Millions of sandboxes. Revenue already covering most costs. And they launched this version of the product just six months before we sat down.

## Selling to the parent and the child

Ivan gave me the simplest analogy I've heard for the dual-audience problem in agentic infrastructure. Think of it like selling children's toys. You need the parent, the developer, to choose the product. And you need the child, the agent, to perform well inside it.

Agents can't yet autonomously discover and adopt infrastructure. So developers still make the buying decision. But once the agent runs, performance matters. Ivan says agents are twice as productive on Daytona versus alternatives. And when things break, he personally gets on calls. The team responds in minutes, not hours.

As someone who talks to 40+ devtools startups a year, Victoria Melnikova can confirm that support quality is consistently one of the most underrated competitive advantages. Ivan gets this instinctively. Even when Daytona screws up, the speed and honesty of their response keeps customers around. Every single customer who had a problem stayed.

## Revenue goes to zero on New Year's Eve

This is the part of the conversation that really stuck with me. Daytona was doing dev environment automation for human developers and large enterprises. $300K ARR. Not bad, especially pre-AI. But by late 2024, inbound requests from agent builders started showing up.

On New Year's Eve, Ivan and his co-founder built a beta without telling the rest of the company. When early users confirmed it was directionally right, they made the call: revenue goes to zero. Thank every old customer. Rebuild from scratch starting February 1st.

> "Should have done it sooner. It's always like, you should have done that sooner."

They hit the old $300K ARR in the first month of the new product. And then kept doubling. Here's the thing. Ivan had been through this timing lesson before. His first company, Codeanywhere, was a browser-based IDE built in 2009. A decade before Replit, before Codespaces. Being early didn't help. This time, the timing was right, and he knew it in his gut.

## Play to your genius zone

Daytona's go-to-market is almost entirely in-person events. Ivan ran a conference company before. He built it and sold it, so organizing meetups, hackathons, and drinks is second nature for his team. Two people on the GTM team came from that old conference company. They've organized events in stadiums. A San Francisco meetup is easy by comparison.

Ivan is honest about what he's not good at. He can't write. It takes too long and it doesn't feel natural. But getting in a room with people? That's his genius zone. And crucially, almost the entire market of agent builders is concentrated in San Francisco, so the geography works perfectly.

They were promoting the product for three months before it even launched. In hindsight, Ivan says that was one of their smartest moves. But in the moment? Pure leap of faith. I respect that level of conviction.

## 19 years with the same people, racing on a moving train

Ivan has worked with his co-founders for nearly two decades across multiple companies. Twelve of Daytona's 18 employees are people they've worked with for 6 to 20 years. The communication is direct, high-fidelity, zero BS.

> "My co-founder will call me and say, stop talking shit, this is a dumb idea. Or vice versa. There's no fuzziness around it. It's very direct."

That speed of communication matters when you're doubling every month. But Ivan is clear that growing fast isn't enough. You have to outrun the market itself. His analogy is perfect: you're on a moving train (the AI wave), but you're racing competitors on that train. When the market slows down, only number one survives.

When Victoria Melnikova asked what makes him feel great, Ivan's answer surprised me. It wasn't about growth charts or revenue. It's about time. He sees Daytona as a catalyst for buying time, empowering agents to relieve humans of work so they can spend more time with their kids, or on whatever matters most to them. Coming from a guy who admits he hopes to see his kids in the morning and at least put them to bed at night, that hit differently.
