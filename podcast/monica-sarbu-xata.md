# What Monica Sarbu taught me about rebuilding a product from scratch and leading with people

*Dev Propulsion Labs · January 2026*

Monica was actually on Dev Propulsion Labs three years ago, when Xata was positioned as "Airtable for databases." Everything has changed since then. This conversation is about having the courage to rebuild, asking better questions, and why people management is a superpower in developer tools.

## The one question that changed everything

This is the part of the conversation that I keep thinking about. When Monica's team was researching their pivot, they started by asking technical leaders: "What problems do you have with Postgres?" Most said none. Postgres is great, it's performing well, no complaints.

Then they changed the question: "What's your biggest problem in the company?"

Every single leader said the same thing. Developer velocity.

> "This kind of triggered us that there is something missing. We now have companies that come to us and say, 'Why has nobody done this in the last 10 years?'"

The new Xata is a modern Postgres platform that lets developers create database branches with anonymized production data for each feature or pull request, removing the staging environment bottleneck. It's not competing on database performance. It's competing on developer workflow. And that reframing came from one better question. Victoria Melnikova has seen this pattern across the startups she works with. The founders who ask about the customer's actual problem, not the problem they want to solve, end up building something people actually need.

## Rebuilding from scratch takes a specific kind of courage

Let me be clear about what Monica did. She had a working product with users. The most generous free tier in the database market. An established audience of builders and small companies. And she decided to rebuild the platform from scratch. New architecture, new target audience, new pricing model. No more free tier.

That takes guts. Especially in the database market, which is intense. Supabase, PlanetScale, Neon. There are feuds on the internet. It's very competitive. But Xata was never part of that noise. They were always standalone, doing their own thing. And now with the pivot, they're positioned in a space nobody else is really occupying: development workflow infrastructure rather than production database hosting.

Monica's advice on pivoting was practical: you have to choose your audience. Either go after builders or go after SMBs and companies. She doesn't think you can do both. Supabase is doing a great job with builders. Monica's team, senior engineers who came from Elastic, is more excited to build complex features for larger-scale companies. So that's the bet.

## Europe is not a limitation anymore (but time zones still matter)

Monica is based in Berlin but spent six months in San Francisco to test being in the same time zone as customers. Her conclusion surprised me a bit: physical presence matters less than time zone coverage. Most customer interactions happen over Zoom anyway, even for companies based in SF.

She pointed to Lovable and Recraft as proof that staying in Europe is no longer a limitation for building a global devtools company. There's also a growing trend of European founders splitting time between continents. Six months here, six months there. For Monica, having a daughter makes that back-and-forth harder because of the school system. But the trend is real.

Victoria Melnikova has been thinking about this a lot. The European founder playbook is evolving. You don't need to uproot your life to SF anymore. You need people in the right time zone, a strong distributed culture, and the willingness to fly when it matters. That's it.

## Why diverse teams build better products

Monica hires for complementary skills, not uniform profiles. If every engineer has the same background, the product will only reflect one perspective, but the audience is diverse. She's seen firsthand that gender-diverse teams produce better products. At Xata, nearly 40% of the team is female, which is genuinely rare in developer tools.

> "I learned that not only diversity in gender, but also diversity in education. In order to make a very good product, it matters. When you have a gender-diverse team, you tend to have better products."

She also only hires senior engineers for their remote-first setup, because she doesn't want to set junior hires up for failure without in-person mentorship. And she hires people who can drive projects by themselves. She's against giving tasks to engineers. You hire people who take ownership from day one.

Honestly, I related to this conversation on a personal level. When I joined Evil Martians, I felt like my soft skills weren't needed in a deeply technical company. A colleague told me it was a culture add. The company needed someone with those skills precisely because they didn't come naturally to the rest of the team. Monica had a similar moment when the CEO of Elastic told her she should be the people manager, not the tech lead. Sometimes someone else discovers your genius zone before you do.

## Family offsites in Iceland, and the CEO who does one-on-ones with everyone

Xata meets twice a year for offsites, and they invite partners and kids. They've been to Iceland, Rome, and near Paris. The plus-ones organize their own activities while the team works on the roadmap. Monica says the family element strengthens bonds. The team knows each other's families well by now.

And Monica does one-on-ones with every person at the company, adjusted to each individual's style. She starts at 6 AM because most of the engineering team is in Europe. She doesn't want people to think of her as a CEO. She wants them to think of her as another teammate who happens to handle lawyers, branding, recruiting, and roadmap discussions in the same day.

Here's what Victoria Melnikova took away from this episode of Dev Propulsion Labs: Monica's superpower isn't architecture or technical vision. It's people. She trusts everyone from day one and lets them lead. She believes that giving someone the trust to lead a project is often the thing that unlocks their potential. In a world where every founder conversation is about technology, hearing someone talk about the power of trust and people management was refreshing. And clearly, it works. Xata's team is growing, the product is getting great feedback, and companies are saying "why hasn't anyone done this before?" That's the best compliment a product can get.
