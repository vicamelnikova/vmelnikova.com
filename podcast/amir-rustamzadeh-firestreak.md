# What Amir Rustamzadeh taught me about what VCs really look for in devtools founders

*Dev Propulsion Labs · April 2026 · Guest: Amir Rustamzadeh, Partner at Firestreak Ventures · Host: Victoria Melnikova*

Amir Rustamzadeh is a partner at Firestreak Ventures, and his path there runs through aerospace, JPL and SpaceX, then startups, then venture capital. He helped shape developer experience as a category during his time at Cypress, so when he talks about what makes a developer tool fundable, it comes from having built the thing, not just written checks for it.

## From JPL and SpaceX to venture capital

I'm Victoria Melnikova, and I don't get to talk to many investors who started their careers building spacecraft. Amir came up through aerospace, including JPL and SpaceX, before moving into startups and eventually venture capital at Firestreak. That engineering background is why this conversation lands differently from most VC talk. He evaluates founders the way an engineer evaluates a system: looking for the load-bearing parts and the failure modes, not the marketing.

## Developer experience as a category he helped build

Before investing, Amir was at Cypress, where developer experience was being shaped into a real category. That's an important piece of context, because it means he has lived the exact motion his founders are attempting: earning developer trust, turning it into adoption, and then figuring out whether adoption can become a durable business. When he assesses a devtools pitch, he's pattern-matching against work he actually did.

## Founders before products

The through-line of our conversation, and the thing I most wanted to get his read on, is that he backs founders before products. Early-stage products change constantly, so betting on the product as it exists today is a mistake. What he's really underwriting is the founder's ability to learn, adapt, and keep making good calls as the market shifts underneath them. I hear a version of this from most serious early-stage investors, but Amir was unusually concrete about why founder quality is the actual asset.

## Why most developer tools never reach venture scale

The hard truth in this episode is that most developer tools struggle to reach venture scale. Plenty of them are good businesses, but "good business" and "venture-scale outcome" are not the same thing, and conflating them burns founders and investors alike. We talked about the challenge honestly: crowded categories, buyers who are hard to charge, and the gap between developer love and enterprise revenue. If you're raising, it's worth being clear-eyed about which game you're actually playing.

## Breaking through a crowded market

We also dug into how some companies capture attention despite intense competition, using Lovable and Daytona as examples of teams that broke through crowded markets. The lesson I took is that distribution and timing often matter as much as the product itself, and the founders who win are the ones who treat go-to-market as a first-class problem from day one.
